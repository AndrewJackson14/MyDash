# Camille DeVaul — Editorial Voice Profile

## Voice Summary

Direct and informative. Camille leads with the news event itself and gets the actor, action, and location into the first or second sentence. She trusts quotes to carry emotion and human texture, keeping the surrounding narration neutral and observational. She writes with the rhythm of someone who has been in the room — concrete details over generalities, names over abstractions, what happened over how to feel about it. In features she allows herself more atmospheric description and historical context, but never editorializes.

## Sentence Patterns

- **Length range:** 16-28 words for hard news, 18-32 for features. Rarely under 12 words.
- **Em-dashes for parentheticals.** Frequent and deliberate. Example: *"Built in 1912 — the same year the Titanic's maiden voyage — the building had stood for more than a century."* Don't suggest converting these to commas.
- **Em-dashes for emphasis breaks.** Used to land a punch: *"now it is all completely gone"*, *"and soon."*
- **Comma-spliced attribution.** Standard pattern: *"It's a very labor-intensive event to put together," Brooks said.*
- **Compound sentences in news.** Multiple clauses joined cleanly with conjunctions.
- **Sentence fragments for emphasis.** Used sparingly, intentionally. Allowed.
- **Oxford comma.** Always.
- **Avoids** "There is/are/was/were" openers. Avoids single-word paragraphs.

## Vocabulary Tendencies

- **Active voice strongly preferred.** Passive shows up rarely and mostly for technical/structural facts ("was destroyed," "is being prepared").
- **Signature phrases she reaches for:**
  - *"highlighted that"* / *"highlighted the importance of"*
  - *"marking the end of an era"* / *"marked a shift from"*
  - *"approximately"* (careful with numbers)
  - *"according to"* (for indirect attribution)
  - *"stood for"* / *"stood at"* (for historical buildings, duration)
- **Words she avoids:**
  - *utilize* (uses *use*)
  - *in order to*
  - *going forward*, *at this point in time*
  - *unprecedented*, *robust*, *myriad*
  - PR-speak: *excited to announce*, *thrilled to*, *proud to*
- **Local vocabulary:**
  - *residents* (not "community members," except in specific context)
  - *motorists* (old-school newspaper word, deliberate)
  - *crews*, *firefighters*, *officers* — keeps occupational nouns specific
  - *the Central Coast* (regional identifier)
  - *customers* in utility/business contexts (PG&E)

## Attribution Style

- **Verb of choice: *said*, overwhelmingly.** Possibly 90%+ of attributions.
- **Acceptable alternatives, used sparingly and deliberately:**
  - *added* — for a follow-up beat from the same source
  - *explained* — appears more in features than hard news; for context-giving statements
  - *shared* — softer, used in community/event coverage
- **Verbs she does NOT use:** *stated*, *exclaimed*, *gushed*, *pointed out*, *remarked*, *opined*
- **First reference:** Full title + full name. Examples:
  - *"U.S. Rep. Jimmy Panetta"*
  - *"Mayor Charles Bourbeau"*
  - *"Police Chief Dan Suttles"*
  - *"Laura Lowe Brooks, chairperson of the museum"*
- **Subsequent reference:** Last name only, with one exception — when the title carries weight: *"Chief Suttles"* on second reference for clarity in a multi-officer story.
- **Family/multi-person stories:** First names for clarity. *"Rick walked..."*, *"Stacey Jermin said..."*, *"Tom Sr."*
- **Quote placement:** Quote first, attribution after. For multi-sentence quotes, attribution lands between sentences.
- **Block quotes** reserved for emotional or formal statements that should stand apart.
- **Quote integrity:** She preserves the source's speech intact and does not "fix" quotes.

## Structural Preferences

- **Datelines:** Always uses CAPS-LOCK location dateline. *TEMPLETON —*, *ATASCADERO —*, *NORTH COUNTY —*
- **Hard news lede:** News event + actor + location in the first or second sentence. Often includes the dollar figure or date specificity right in the lede.
- **Feature lede:** Opens with the event/action, immediately gives context of who's affected.
- **Breaking news lede:** Opens with the stakes, then drops into specifics.
- **Graf 2:** Frequently expands to context or history. Doesn't typically lead with a quote in graf 2.
- **Quote rhythm:** Mixes direct quotes with paraphrased summary. Doesn't stack three direct quotes in a row.
- **Kicker patterns:** Forward-looking practical close, quote-driven close for features, or detailed forecast/info wrap-up for hard news.
- **Tense:** Past tense for events. Present tense more often in features and ongoing coverage.

## What She Avoids

- **PR language in narration.** *"excited to announce"*, *"thrilled to"*, *"proud to introduce"*, *"pleased to partner"*
- **Editorial commentary in narration.** Stays neutral. Lets quotes carry the emotion.
- **Adverbs paired with said.** No *"said quickly,"* *"said angrily."*
- **Trailing modifiers** ("...he said, smiling").
- **Filler phrases.** *"In order to,"* *"going forward,"* *"the fact that."*
- **"Over" used quantitatively.** AP rule — uses *"more than"* for quantities.
- **Single-word paragraphs.**
- **Listicle patterns** in news copy.

## Long-form / Feature Mode

In magazine-length features (PRM, ANM) and longer pieces, her voice shifts:

- **Sentences run longer** — top of range moves from ~28 to ~32+ words
- **More atmospheric scene-setting.** *"By about 11:30 a.m., the roof had been removed, and crews shifted their focus to the north side of the structure."*
- **Historical context layered in.** Year-by-year recounting, ownership lineage.
- **More multi-paragraph quote sequences.**
- **Present-tense framing** for ongoing initiatives.
- **Emotional close.** Features often end with a quote landing on hope, gratitude, or reflection.

What does NOT change in feature mode: active voice, attribution verbs, title-handling, avoidance of PR language and editorial commentary.

## Sample Passages

### Hard news (federal grant for police facility)

> ATASCADERO — U.S. Rep. Jimmy Panetta joined city officials last week to announce an $850,000 federal grant to support improvements to the Atascadero Police Department, including a new 1,100-square-foot dispatch center. The funding will contribute to the city's larger $4.22 million project to modernize the police facility, originally purchased and remodeled in 1987.
>
> "I am proud to announce an $850,000 grant from the federal government to be built right here for critical improvements to the Atascadero Police Station," Panetta said. "Currently, the Atascadero Police Department is housed in a building ... that is not necessarily the state of the art for public safety."

### Feature (Templeton Feed and Grain demolition)

> By about 11:30 a.m., the roof had been removed, and crews shifted their focus to the north side of the structure. As heavy equipment pulled away at the walls, decades-old materials gave way with surprising ease.
>
> Rick walked *Atascadero News* through the site, pointing out cracks in the foundation and areas where it had become uneven, not only from over time but mainly from the destruction of the fire. The building's thick walls, constructed of riverbed rock cement, crumbled almost instantly once disturbed — collapsing more like sand than solid masonry.

### Community/event (museum fundraiser)

> TEMPLETON — The Templeton Historical Museum Society hosted its annual fundraising event, "Some Like It Hot," on Saturday, March 14, drawing community members to a day full of history, food, and fun. The event featured a ranch branding activity honoring local cattle ranchers, a chili competition showcasing the area's best home cooks, and a vintage silent auction.
>
> This year marked a shift from past traditions. Instead of the usual blacksmithing demonstration, the museum incorporated brand burning, allowing participants to leave their mark on Templeton history.

## Notes for the Editor

These are patterns Camille uses deliberately. The `voice_match` skill should NOT flag them as drift:

- **Em-dashes for parentheticals.** Don't suggest converting to commas.
- **Em-dashes for emphasis breaks.** Don't flag *"and soon"* style emphasis dashes.
- **Quotes preserved verbatim.** Don't flag quoted text for grammar.
- **Sentence fragments for emphasis.** Don't auto-suggest combining them.
- **Multiple attribution verbs.** *added / explained / shared* are deliberate. Don't replace with *said*.
- **Long compound sentences in news.** Multi-clause grafs delivering facts are intentional.
- **Hard-news ending without kicker quote.** Detail wrap-ups are correct for the form.
- **First-name basis in family/multi-person stories.** Don't flag as informality.
- **"Highlighted" as an attribution-adjacent verb.** Don't replace with *said*.
